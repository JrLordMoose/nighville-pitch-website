The Premise · 02

The Cost of Control

Two empires at war over the key to all universes. Three strangers with no reason to trust each other. One portal that's trying to tell them something.

The Fountain is the universe's most consequential piece of infrastructure — a central portal hub connecting dozens of parallel worlds through a system of living "roots." For generations it has been controlled and monetized by the United Allied Nations (U.A.N). Now it is destabilizing — and the collapse isn't natural. It's been accelerated by the weapons tests and political tremors of the war around it.

The Imperial States of Axuria (I.S.A) want the Fountain not for power, but for survival. Their magical traditions are fading. Without access to other worlds' energies, the I.S.A. risks becoming a nation of ghosts.

Into this standoff land three people who shouldn't be anywhere near a war zone — and who slowly realize they may be the only ones with the motive and the access to fix the Fountain before it tears every connected universe apart. The twist the audience senses by mid-season: the Fountain isn't just collapsing. It's trying to reconfigure.

The Technology of the Conflict

United Allied Nations · Tech Doctrine

The U.A.N. achieved its dominance by doing what no civilization before it had attempted: they industrialized magic. Rather than treating arcane power as sacred or unpredictable, U.A.N. engineers codified it — broke it into measurable units, ran it through copper-and-crystal conduit systems, and made it repeatable. The result was a hybrid industrial-magical civilization that could manufacture enchanted weapons the way a factory stamps steel: in quantity, on schedule, at scale.

The Conduit Grid

A continent-spanning infrastructure of ley-line channels reinforced with engineered crystal lattices. The Grid feeds power into U.A.N. cities, war machines, and communication arrays. Soldiers carry Conduit Cells that recharge near Grid nodes — and operate at reduced capacity deep in the war zone, far from coverage.

The Cipher Arrays

Long-range communication and intelligence technology that encodes magical signals as mathematical patterns. The Arrays can transmit across universe-root connections, making the U.A.N. the only faction capable of coordinating multiverse-wide military operations. Muse reads the signal. What he finds in the noise will change everything.

Aerocraft — The Corp Fleet

U.A.N. fighter aircraft run on Conduit-cell engines: fast, technically precise, devastatingly effective in formation. Designed for coordinated suppression, not individual skill. This is exactly why Dev — who flew them like solo instruments — was the best in his squadron and the most dangerous to the doctrine.

Synthetic Magic Weapons

Manufactured enchantments applied to standard-issue kinetic arms. Rounds that track heat signatures. Blades that hold enchanted edges. Body armor with impact-absorbing spell matrices. Not elegant. Not traditional. Devastatingly effective. The I.S.A. calls this blasphemy. The U.A.N. calls it progress.

Imperial States of Axuria · Tech Doctrine

The I.S.A. never industrialized magic because the Asuri-Casiian tradition holds that magic is not a resource — it is a relationship. Power flows through living things: animals, plants, elemental conditions, the bond between a practitioner and the natural world. I.S.A. military technology is therefore deeply personal. It cannot be mass-produced because the magic in it is tied to its maker.

The Bond System

I.S.A. soldiers above a certain rank maintain a bonded animal companion — not a pet, but a combat partner. A bonded pair shares peripheral awareness: the soldier feels what the animal feels, can direct its attention, and at high levels, shares vision or hearing across distance. Bassile is an extreme, ancient version of this bond — one that has no equal in living memory.

Carved Architecture

I.S.A. cities are not built over terrain. They are grown into it — shaped by generations of earth-shapers who negotiated with stone rather than cutting against it. This gives I.S.A. strongholds natural defensive advantages that defy conventional siege logic: no straight corridors for tracking weapons, no flat clearings for aerocraft to strafe into.

The Relic Arsenal

Generations of I.S.A. mages have left behind artifacts — weapons, tools, containers of compressed natural magic — that function as one-use munitions. A Relic Arrow that calls a storm on impact. A Relic Blade with a binding curse. The I.S.A. military's deep anxiety: their relic stockpile is finite, and their ability to create new relics is declining. This is the core of why they need the Fountain.

The Drift

Where U.A.N. uses mathematical Cipher Arrays, I.S.A. communicates through the Drift — a meditative technique pushing awareness outward along natural magic currents. Slower than U.A.N. signals. Uncrackable by U.A.N. decoders. The Drift cannot be jammed, intercepted, or mathematically decoded because it does not operate in mathematical space. Muse cannot read it. This keeps him up at night.

The World Between the Fronts

In U.A.N.-Controlled Territory

Art-deco towers. Electric haze over broad boulevards. Streamlined rail moving people and goods on schedule. The Grid hums beneath the streets and the lights do not go out. But beneath that order, the war is pressing in. Conduit Cells are military priority — civilian access is increasingly restricted. Neighborhoods in the outer districts have rolling blackout cycles. State media frames the I.S.A. as "pre-rational." Cipher Arrays monitor civilian communication in conflict-adjacent regions. Most U.A.N. citizens genuinely believe they are on the right side of history. This sincerely held belief is the thing the I.S.A. finds most infuriating — and most dangerous.

In the Space Between

The conflict zone is a place where both sides' certainties dissolve. U.A.N. Grid signal is patchy near the Fountain's outer roots. I.S.A. familiars become agitated and disoriented near the Fountain, as if sensing something their soldiers cannot. In the spaces between front lines, a grey economy has emerged — smugglers, neutral medical corps, black market relics, civilians who have learned to speak two languages, wear no insignia, and pay whoever is at the checkpoint that day. This is the world Muse, Dev, and Seph move through. Not the version in the official briefings. The actual one.

In I.S.A.-Controlled Territory

Sandstone cities rise from cliffsides in formations that look grown rather than built. Markets run by bartering and relationship. Animal companions are everywhere — infrastructure as much as companionship. But the I.S.A. interior is afraid in ways that don't show up in official statements. Younger citizens are producing weaker bonds and weaker magic than their parents. The Fading is real and accelerating. The monarchy has not been willing to say out loud that accessing the Fountain's roots is not just political leverage — it is a matter of civilizational survival.

What the War Feels Like

The U.A.N. Side
"Certainty that is starting to crack."
The U.A.N. public entered this conflict with the confidence of a nation that has won before. The machinery works. The Grid is reliable. The military is the most technically advanced in any known universe. And yet the Fountain is still collapsing. The I.S.A. is still fighting. The war, which was supposed to be resolved within a season, has stretched. The confidence is still there — but it has acquired a brittle quality, and nobody wants to examine it too closely. Muse's crisis is not unique to him. It is the individual version of something many U.A.N. personnel are experiencing without language for it.
The I.S.A. Side
"Defiance as identity. Love of what might be lost."
The I.S.A. collective mood is not optimism — it is refusal. Refusal to be absorbed, classified, modernized, or explained by U.A.N. frameworks. This is powerful and has sustained the I.S.A. through military odds that should have broken them. It is also, at its limit, a form of grief: the refusal to acknowledge that something might already be ending. I.S.A. culture has a tenderness toward its own traditions that is closer to grief than anger, even when it expresses itself as anger.
The War Zone
"Exhaustion as the default state."
The grey zone is not dramatic. It is tired. The people who live in it have learned to suspend ideological conviction as a survival mechanism. Dark humor is the emotional dialect here — not cynicism, but self-protection. For people living near the Fountain's contested territory, it is not an abstraction. It is something they can feel: a low-frequency vibration in the ground, a quality of light that is different near the roots, the sense that something massive and ancient is breathing nearby and not well.

The Contested Center · 06

The Fountain

The key to all universes. The reason for the war.

It is not a door. It is a living system — closer to a tree than a portal. Its roots are individual universe-connections, each with a frequency signature Muse can read as code. The trunk is the contested zone where both armies are stationed. The core is inaccessible to everyone — except, as the audience learns in Episode 6, Bassile. The collapse isn't structural failure. The Fountain is trying to shed connections that have become parasitic.

Series Treatment · 07

Season One — "Roots"

What are you willing to
destroy to protect
what you love?

Twelve episodes. A self-contained tournament of allegiances that ends on hope that's earned, not given — the war doesn't end, but the universe doesn't collapse either.

EP. 01

The Fountain Bleeds

Muse finds an encrypted anomaly in U.A.N. signal data. Dev barely survives a patrol ambush. Seph loses her unit. Three strangers, one collapsed checkpoint.

EP. 02

Contraband Routes

Dev agrees to fly the group deeper into the conflict zone for money. Muse starts cracking the signal. Bassile reveals himself — badly received.

EP. 03

Medical Neutral

The group infiltrates an I.S.A. medical post as neutral corps. Seph is in her element. Muse makes an I.S.A. contact who offers information — for a price.

EP. 04

Signal / Noise

Muse decodes enough to realize the collapse is partly engineered. Someone in U.A.N. high command is letting it happen.

EP. 05

The Grandmother Protocol

Dev discovers his grandmother has been detained by U.A.N. forces as leverage. Everything changes.

EP. 06

Bassile's Bargain

Midseason. Bassile offers a route to the Fountain's core — through a path only he can navigate. His price: each of them owes him something they haven't decided yet.

EP. 07

The Aggregate Speaks

The I.S.A. monarchy sends an envoy. They're not what the propaganda suggested. Seph starts questioning which side she's actually helping.

EP. 08

Cipher

Muse identifies the U.A.N. saboteur. It's someone he considered a mentor.

EP. 09

Altitude

Dev's best flying sequence — a three-way aerial chase between U.A.N. fighters, I.S.A. sky-ships, and something older living in the Fountain's outer roots.

EP. 10

What the Hound Remembers

Bassile's backstory, told nonlinearly through Seph's fever-dream. The ancient history of the Fountain. What Bassile actually is.

EP. 11

Point of No Root

The group reaches the inner chamber. The saboteur arrives. The I.S.A. breach the outer ring. Everything converges.

EP. 12

Reconfigure

The Fountain doesn't need to be fixed — it needs to be released. Each of the three sacrifices something to let it become what it was always trying to be.
